// CRAFT & PHILOSOPHY
► On quality and debugging

I treat debugging as the purest form of engineering — you're building a mental model of a system, forming hypotheses, and testing them against reality. Every bug is a gap in your understanding, and closing that gap makes you a better engineer. My approach is systematic: reproduce, isolate, understand the mechanism, fix the root cause, add the regression test, and ask "why wasn't this caught earlier?" The last question is where systemic improvements come from.

► On abstraction

Abstraction is leverage — but every layer of indirection is also a leak surface, a debugging tax, and a cognitive load. I've seen too many codebases where "clean architecture" meant six layers of indirection that made a simple data fetch require opening 14 files. I reach for abstraction when it removes duplication or enables testing, not because it's the fashionable pattern. The right abstraction saves time; the wrong one costs more than it saves, forever.

► On working at scale

Scale isn't just about requests per second — it's about complexity management. At the Belastingdienst (Dutch tax authority), "scale" meant millions of citizens depending on correct data processing with zero tolerance for error. At Rabobank, it meant financial transactions where a bug costs real money. In life sciences (SkinConsult), it meant regulatory compliance where a data leak costs your license. Every one of these environments taught me that scale is a systemic property: you can't bolt it on after shipping.
