// ENGINEERING PHILOSOPHY

Standards before tools. I don't believe in imposing uniformity for its own sake. The goal is enough consistency that an engineer from Team A can review a PR from Team B and immediately understand the patterns, without either team losing the autonomy to pick the right tool for their problem. That's a balance I've struck three times — at the Belastingdienst (introducing test automation + structured logging across 4 teams), at Rabobank (peer review as a cultural standard, not a checkbox), and at SkinConsult (code review standards across 3 holding companies).

AI is a craft tool, not a crutch. I use Claude daily — for architecture exploration, code generation, review, migration scripts, and documentation. Every output goes through the same quality gates as human code: audit-trail checks, tenant isolation verification, data-boundary validation. The multiplier isn't in the generation — it's in the operator discipline to validate what gets produced. That's the same mindset Synthesia's product engineers would need working alongside R&D: trust but verify, at speed.

Commercial outcomes drive architecture. Every technical decision I've made in the past 10 years ties to a business metric. When I refactored a monolith into modular services, it wasn't for architectural purity — it was because regulatory deadlines required parallel delivery threads. When I eliminated 319k lines of code in 4 months, it wasn't a cleanup — it was reducing cognitive load so the team could ship features faster. Engineering exists to serve the product.
